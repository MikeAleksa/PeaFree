About

My vet cautioned me against grain-free diets after a recent FDA investigation into these foods. The FDA's study indicates some of the main ingredients in these foods may be responsible for a rise in cases of Canine Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM), a type of heart disease in dogs.

In my search to find a new food, I found it difficult to identify which foods met the FDA's new guidelines without looking at each and every ingredient, so I set out to solve the problem programmatically. This website is the result of my efforts - an easy way to search for an acceptable dog food, or to check your current food.

Just go ahead and search to see if your food follows the guidelines, or use the advanced search to find an acceptable food for dogs of any size and lifestage.

The information for this website was scraped form Chewy.com, and ingredients are examined according to the guidelines outlined by the FDA. Foods containing discouraged ingredients are marked, and these ingredients are highlighted in the food's details page.

The FDA's announcement can be found here and a summary can be found here.


Key points from the FDA's summary:

The FDA is cautioning against feeding dogs food that contains legumes, pulses (seeds of legumes), and/or (sweet) potatoes as main ingredients in the food.

Main ingredients are defined as ingredients that appear in a food's ingredient list before the first appearance of a vitamin or mineral ingredient.

Common legumes include peas, beans, lentils, chickpeas, soybeans and peanuts. Pulses are dry edible seeds of certain legume plants. Examples include dried beans, dried peas, chickpeas and lentils.

Rice is a grain, not a legume. The current reports do not suggest there is any link between diets with rice and DCM in dogs.

Grain-free diets are not necessarily specifically implicated by the study, but recently the proportion of legumes, pulses, and potatoes have increased significantly in these diets.
